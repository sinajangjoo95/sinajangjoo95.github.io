Research

How emerging technologies and sustainability transitions become governable.

I am a public administration and policy scholar studying early-phase governance under institutional mismatch. My work develops Contested Rule Formation to explain how actors classify new problems, settle authority, build venues, and create procedural rules before stable policy systems fully exist.

Across AI governance, autonomous systems, AI infrastructure, clean energy transitions, collaborative governance, nonprofit and civil society action, and urban governance, I study the upstream politics of governance.

Featured Framework

Contested Rule Formation

Contested Rule Formation explains how governance becomes possible when inherited institutions do not map cleanly onto emerging socio-technical practices. It focuses on the upstream phase of policy development, when actors are still struggling to define the policy object, locate authority, settle venues, and build procedural rules.

Many policy process theories explain change within relatively established policy systems. CRF focuses on an earlier moment: when the system itself is unsettled.

Institutional Mismatch

What problem does CRF address?

Emerging technologies and sustainability transitions often become visible before institutions are ready to govern them. New practices, infrastructures, or risks do not fit inherited categories, agency mandates, enforcement routines, or policy venues.

RecognitionAuthorityEnforcement CapacityVenue ClarityInterpretive Frames
01Incipient Recognition

A new issue becomes visible, but institutions have not yet classified it clearly.

02Exploratory Tactics

Actors experiment with pilots, guidance documents, voluntary standards, lawsuits, hearings, or local ordinances.

03Rule Crystallization

Some classifications, venues, and procedural templates begin to stabilize.

04Consolidation, Backlash, or Fragmentation

The emerging rule system may become durable, face backlash, shift venues, or fragment across jurisdictions.

Upstream AI Governance

AI governance is often discussed through ethics, risk management, technical safety, or adoption capacity. My work asks a prior public-administration question: when does AI become institutionally governable?

Institutional Readiness for Public-Sector AI

Readiness requires classification, venue settlement, documentation rules, procurement standards, review procedures, and correction mechanisms.

Accountability Lag in Autonomous Systems

Embodied AI can disrupt streets, sidewalks, public spaces, and administrative routines before institutions have procedures for review and correction.

AI Infrastructure and Data Centers

AI depends on land, electricity, water, cooling, transmission, tax incentives, zoning approvals, and local permitting.

Procedural Governance

AI governance becomes real through inventories, procurement clauses, audit triggers, complaint channels, appeal rights, and public reporting.

Civil Society Rule Formation

Civil society actors can make AI governance problems visible, produce templates, activate venues, and demand procedural safeguards.

Public Accountability

The goal is to make AI systems reviewable, contestable, accountable, and publicly defensible before adoption becomes routine.

Teaching

Institutions, evidence, implementation, and accountability

My teaching prepares students to analyze public problems through institutions, evidence, implementation, and accountability. I approach public administration and policy as fields concerned not only with what governments and nonprofit organizations should do, but also with how public authority is organized, how decisions are implemented, and how institutions can be designed to serve democratic, equitable, and effective public purposes.

I use cases, policy documents, organizational examples, simulations, applied assignments, and structured discussion to help students move between concepts and real governance problems.

About

Sina Jangjoo is an incoming tenure-track Assistant Professor of Public Administration at The University of Texas at Tyler and a PhD Candidate in Public Administration and Policy at Florida State University.

His research sits at the intersection of public administration, policy process, science and technology policy, environmental policy, urban governance, nonprofit studies, and urban planning. His work asks how emerging policy domains become governable under institutional mismatch: how actors classify new problems, locate authority, construct venues, and stabilize procedural rules before mature policy systems exist.

His dissertation examines local clean energy governance in Florida under state preemption and commission-centered electricity regulation. It develops an account of rule location under constrained polycentricity, showing how local governments, nonprofits, and intermediaries construct governable pathways through program-facing and procurement micro-rules when core regulatory authority remains insulated.
